# Renting with Pets in Victoria — Your Complete Rights Guide (2026)

*Last updated March 2026*

**Since March 2020, Victorian landlords can no longer unreasonably refuse a tenant's request to keep a pet. This landmark change under the Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2018 shifted the balance of power firmly in favour of pet-owning renters. If you rent in Victoria and want to keep a pet — or already have one — this guide explains your rights, the application process, what happens if you are refused, and how to protect yourself throughout your tenancy.**

## Background — How the Law Changed

### The Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2018

Victoria's rental pet laws underwent their most significant overhaul in decades through the Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2018. While the legislation was passed in 2018, the pet-related provisions were phased in as part of a broader rollout, with the key changes taking effect on 2 March 2020.

Before this date, landlords had effectively unchecked discretion to refuse pets. Many lease agreements contained blanket "no pets" clauses, and tenants had little practical recourse. The result was that thousands of Victorian renters either gave up beloved pets, rehomed animals when their housing situation changed, or concealed pet ownership in breach of their lease.

The reform was driven by recognition that Victoria's growing renter population — now exceeding 30 percent of all households — included a significant number of pet owners who faced systemic discrimination in the rental market. Animal welfare organisations, tenant advocacy groups, and community campaigners all contributed to the push for change.

### What Changed on 2 March 2020

The core change is straightforward: a landlord cannot unreasonably refuse consent for a tenant to keep a pet at the rental property. This applies to all residential tenancies covered by the Residential Tenancies Act 1997, which includes the vast majority of private rental arrangements in Victoria.

The law does not give tenants an absolute right to keep any pet in any property. It creates a framework where tenants can request permission, landlords must respond within a defined timeframe, refusals must be justified, and unreasonable refusals can be challenged at VCAT.

This shift — from a system where landlords could refuse without reason to one where refusals must be reasonable — is the fundamental change that pet-owning renters need to understand.

### How This Compares to Other States

Victoria was the first Australian state to introduce such comprehensive protections for pet-owning renters. Other states have since moved in similar directions, but Victoria's framework remains the most established and tested through VCAT decisions.

In Queensland, similar reforms took effect in 2022. New South Wales and Western Australia have also progressed reforms, though the specifics differ. For renters moving to Victoria from interstate, it is important to understand that Victorian law provides among the strongest protections for pet owners in the country.

## What "Unreasonable Refusal" Actually Means

### The Legal Test

The Residential Tenancies Act does not provide a rigid checklist for what constitutes reasonable or unreasonable refusal. Instead, it establishes a test of reasonableness that considers the full circumstances of each case. When a refusal is challenged at VCAT, the tribunal weighs multiple factors to determine whether the landlord's decision was reasonable.

This means there is no single answer that applies to every situation. A refusal that is reasonable for one property may be unreasonable for another, depending on the specific facts.

### Factors VCAT Considers

**Type of pet.** The species, breed, size, and temperament of the pet are all relevant. Common household pets — cats, small to medium dogs, fish, caged birds, and small caged animals — are broadly considered reasonable for most residential properties. Exotic animals, livestock, or animals with specific environmental needs face a higher bar.

**Property suitability.** VCAT considers whether the property is physically appropriate for the pet. A large, active dog breed in a small studio apartment may raise legitimate concerns about both the pet's welfare and potential property damage. A cat in a two-bedroom apartment is almost always considered reasonable.

**Strata or body corporate rules.** If the property is subject to owners' corporation rules that regulate (not blanket ban) pets, these rules form part of the reasonableness assessment. More on this in the strata section below.

**Nuisance risk.** If there is a genuine, evidence-based reason to believe the specific pet would cause unreasonable nuisance to neighbours — for example, a breed known for persistent barking in a closely built apartment complex — this can weigh in the landlord's favour. However, speculative concerns about noise are generally not sufficient.

**Number of pets.** Requesting to keep one or two pets is treated differently from requesting to keep six. The number must be reasonable for the property type and size.

### What Is Almost Always Reasonable

Based on VCAT decisions since 2020, the following pet requests are almost always considered reasonable, and refusals in these situations will be difficult for landlords to sustain:

A single cat in an apartment or house. Cats are low-impact pets that cause minimal wear beyond normal use of a property. Unless there are specific strata rules restricting cats (which must themselves be reasonable), refusing a cat is very hard to justify.

A single small to medium dog in a house with a yard. Dogs that have appropriate outdoor space and are registered and vaccinated represent a standard pet-ownership scenario that VCAT generally views favourably.

Fish, caged birds, or small caged animals in any property type. These animals have negligible impact on a property and refusals are essentially indefensible.

### What Might Be Reasonably Refused

A very large or high-energy dog breed in a small apartment without outdoor space, where the dog's welfare and the property's suitability are genuinely in question.

Multiple large animals in a property that cannot reasonably accommodate them, particularly where common areas or neighbours would be significantly affected.

Animals that pose genuine safety concerns, such as certain restricted breeds under Victorian dangerous dog legislation or animals that are not legally permitted as pets in Victoria.

Animals in properties with specific structural limitations, such as a large aquarium in an apartment where the floor loading may be a concern.

### The Burden of Proof

It is important to understand that the burden falls on the landlord to demonstrate that their refusal is reasonable. The tenant does not need to prove their pet will be perfectly behaved. The landlord must show concrete, evidence-based reasons why the specific pet in the specific property is unreasonable.

This is a significant shift from the pre-2020 position and is the reason why most straightforward pet requests now succeed.

## How to Apply to Keep a Pet in Your Rental

### Step 1 — Written Notice to Your Landlord or Agent

To formally request permission to keep a pet, you must provide written notice to your landlord or their property manager. This notice should include the type and breed of pet, a brief description, and any relevant details such as registration numbers, vaccination records, and the pet's temperament.

There is no prescribed form for this notice — an email to your property manager is sufficient. However, putting your request in writing is essential because the 14-day response clock only starts ticking from the date of written notice.

### Step 2 — The 14-Day Response Period

Once your landlord or their agent receives your written notice, they have 14 days to respond. This response must also be in writing. During this 14-day period, the landlord can approve the request, refuse the request with written reasons, or request additional information.

If the landlord requests additional information — for example, asking for a photo of the pet or details about its size — this is generally reasonable and you should cooperate. However, the request for information does not extend the 14-day deadline indefinitely.

### Step 3 — What Happens if There Is No Response

This is one of the most powerful provisions in the legislation: if the landlord fails to respond within 14 days, consent is deemed to have been given. The pet is automatically approved.

This provision exists specifically to prevent landlords from using silence as a de facto refusal. If you send your written notice and hear nothing back within 14 days, you are legally permitted to keep the pet at the property.

However, it is good practice to send a follow-up email at the 14-day mark confirming that you have not received a response and that consent is therefore deemed to have been given. This creates a clear paper trail.

### Step 4 — If You Receive a Refusal

If your landlord refuses your pet request, they must provide written reasons for the refusal. A simple "no" without explanation does not constitute a valid refusal and should be treated as an unreasonable refusal.

Upon receiving a written refusal with reasons, you have two options: accept the refusal, or challenge it at VCAT within 14 days. The VCAT process is covered in detail below.

### Timing Your Request

If you already have a pet when applying for a new rental, it is generally better to disclose the pet during the application process rather than waiting until after you have signed the lease. Our guide on [how to apply for a rental in Victoria](/guides/how-to-apply-rental-victoria) covers the full application process in detail. While legally you can make the request at any point during your tenancy, transparency upfront builds a better relationship with your landlord or property manager.

For tenants who want to acquire a pet during an existing tenancy, you can submit your written notice at any time. There is no requirement to wait until your lease is up for renewal. Your rights apply equally during a fixed-term lease and a month-to-month arrangement. Understanding your broader [renter rights in Victoria](/guides/renter-rights-victoria-guide) will help you navigate these situations with confidence.

## Bond and Financial Implications

### No Separate Pet Bond in Victoria

One of the clearest provisions in Victorian rental law is that landlords cannot charge a separate pet bond or pet deposit. This is explicitly prohibited under the Residential Tenancies Act.

Your bond remains capped at a maximum of four weeks' rent (or one month's rent for properties renting above a prescribed threshold), regardless of whether you have pets. Any landlord or agent who requests an additional payment specifically for pets is acting unlawfully.

### How Pet Damage Is Handled at End of Tenancy

While there is no separate pet bond, landlords can claim for genuine pet damage from your regular bond at the end of the tenancy. This is an important distinction — having a pet does not give you a free pass on damage.

Pet damage that goes beyond normal wear and tear includes scratched doors or window frames, stained or damaged carpets from pet accidents, damaged gardens from digging, and chewed fixtures or fittings.

Normal wear and tear, which cannot be claimed against your bond, includes minor carpet wear in areas where a pet regularly walked, general dirt that can be addressed through standard cleaning, and minor scuffing on hard floors consistent with normal use.

The key test is whether the damage would not have occurred without the pet's presence and whether it exceeds what would be expected from normal residential use.

### Fumigation and End-of-Lease Cleaning

For properties with carpet, landlords can reasonably require that tenants arrange professional flea treatment or fumigation at the end of the tenancy if a pet has been kept on the premises. This is a legitimate condition and typically costs between $100 and $250 depending on the property size.

Landlords can also expect that general end-of-lease cleaning accounts for pet-related cleaning needs, such as removing pet hair from carpets and ensuring outdoor areas are free of animal waste.

These are not additional bond charges — they are legitimate cleaning and maintenance expectations that form part of the standard end-of-tenancy process. Understanding how [bond works in Victoria](/guides/bond-victoria-guide) is essential for all renters, particularly pet owners.

### Protecting Yourself Financially

The most effective financial protection for pet-owning renters is thorough documentation. When you move in, photograph every room, surface, and fixture in detail as part of your condition report. Pay particular attention to carpets, doors, window frames, gardens, and any area where pet damage is commonly claimed.

Throughout your tenancy, address any pet damage promptly. A scratch on a door that is repaired immediately is far less costly than one that is left to worsen over the full lease term. Many minor issues can be addressed with inexpensive repairs that prevent larger bond claims later.

## What Landlords Can Require From Pet Owners

### Reasonable Conditions on Pet Keeping

While landlords cannot unreasonably refuse a pet, they can impose reasonable conditions on pet keeping during the tenancy. These conditions must be genuinely connected to property maintenance, neighbour amenity, or animal welfare — they cannot be used as a backdoor to discourage pet ownership.

### Common Reasonable Conditions

**No nuisance.** Landlords can require that your pet does not cause unreasonable nuisance to neighbours. This includes excessive barking, aggressive behaviour in common areas, and fouling of shared spaces.

**Responsibility for damage.** You are responsible for any damage your pet causes beyond normal wear and tear. This is not so much a condition as a statement of existing law, but landlords often include it explicitly.

**Fumigation or flea treatment.** For carpeted properties, landlords can require that you arrange professional flea treatment at the end of your tenancy. This must be at your expense and is a widely accepted condition.

**Pet to remain within the property boundaries.** Landlords can reasonably require that pets are contained within the property — for example, that a dog does not roam the neighbourhood or that a cat is kept indoors in an apartment setting.

**Compliance with local council regulations.** Your pet must be registered with the relevant local council and comply with any applicable council regulations regarding pet ownership, including leash laws and noise restrictions.

### Conditions That Are Not Reasonable

Landlords cannot impose conditions that effectively make pet ownership impractical or that go beyond what is genuinely necessary. Examples of unreasonable conditions include requiring professional carpet cleaning at specified intervals during the tenancy (rather than just at the end), demanding the pet be kept outside at all times, requiring specific pet insurance or liability coverage beyond what the law mandates, and imposing size or weight limits that are not connected to genuine property suitability concerns.

If you believe a condition is unreasonable, you can raise this with your landlord or agent in the first instance, and escalate to VCAT if necessary.

### Ongoing Obligations During the Tenancy

Throughout your tenancy, you have an ongoing obligation to ensure your pet does not cause damage or unreasonable nuisance. This is not specific to the pet legislation — it falls under the general tenant obligations in the Residential Tenancies Act.

In practice, this means keeping your pet well-cared-for, cleaning up after them, managing barking or noise issues, and promptly addressing any damage. A well-maintained property with a well-behaved pet gives landlords no grounds for complaint.

## The VCAT Process if Your Request Is Refused

### When to Apply to VCAT

If your landlord refuses your pet request and you believe the refusal is unreasonable, you can apply to the Victorian Civil and Administrative Tribunal (VCAT) to challenge the decision. You should apply within 14 days of receiving the written refusal, though VCAT may accept late applications in some circumstances.

Before going to VCAT, consider whether the refusal might actually be reasonable. If your landlord has provided detailed, specific reasons that relate to genuine property suitability concerns, it may be worth having a conversation to see if a compromise is possible before commencing a tribunal process.

### How to Apply

Applications to VCAT are made through the Residential Tenancies List. You can apply online through the VCAT website or in person at a VCAT registry. The application fee is approximately $65 as of 2026, though this is subject to change and fee waivers are available for eligible applicants.

Your application should include a copy of your written pet request, the landlord's written refusal with their stated reasons, any supporting information about your pet (registration, vaccination records, photos, references from previous landlords), and a brief statement explaining why you believe the refusal is unreasonable.

### What Happens at the Hearing

VCAT hearings for pet disputes are generally conducted as short hearings, often lasting 30 to 60 minutes. They can be held in person at a VCAT venue or by phone or video conference.

At the hearing, both you and the landlord (or their representative) will have the opportunity to present your case. VCAT will consider the factors outlined earlier — type of pet, property suitability, strata rules, nuisance risk, and overall reasonableness.

You do not need a lawyer for a VCAT hearing, though you are welcome to bring one. Many tenants represent themselves successfully. Tenant advocacy services, including the Tenants Union of Victoria, can provide advice and sometimes representation.

### Likely Outcomes

Based on VCAT decisions since 2020, pet requests for common household animals in suitable properties have a strong success rate at VCAT. Landlords who refuse cats in apartments, dogs in houses with yards, or small caged animals in any property type have generally had their refusals overturned.

If VCAT finds the refusal unreasonable, it will order the landlord to consent to the pet. The landlord may also be required to pay the tenant's VCAT application fee.

If VCAT finds the refusal reasonable, the tenant's request will not be approved for that specific property. However, this does not prevent the tenant from requesting a different type of pet or reapplying if circumstances change.

### Retaliation Protections

Victorian law includes strong protections against retaliatory action by landlords. If you challenge a pet refusal at VCAT, your landlord cannot issue a notice to vacate, increase your rent, or reduce services in retaliation. Any such action can itself be challenged at VCAT, with significant consequences for the landlord.

These protections are critical because they ensure tenants can exercise their pet rights without fear of losing their home. If you experience what you believe is retaliatory action, contact the Tenants Union of Victoria immediately and document everything.

## Strata and Body Corporate Rules

### The Relationship Between Pet Laws and Owners' Corporation Rules

If your rental property is an apartment or townhouse within a strata-titled development, the owners' corporation (OC) may have rules that relate to pets. The interaction between these rules and the Residential Tenancies Act is one of the more nuanced areas of Victorian pet rental law.

The key principle is that an OC cannot impose a blanket ban on pets. Any OC rules regarding pets must be reasonable restrictions rather than outright prohibitions. This principle was established even before the 2020 rental reforms and has been reinforced by subsequent VCAT and Supreme Court decisions.

### What OC Rules Can Do

Owners' corporations can impose reasonable restrictions that manage the practical aspects of pet ownership in a shared building. Examples of reasonable OC pet rules include requiring pets to be on a leash or carried in common areas, designating specific lifts for pet use during particular hours, requiring pet owners to clean up after their animals immediately, setting limits on the number of pets per lot that reflect the building's capacity, and requiring that pets do not access certain shared facilities like rooftop gardens or swimming pools.

### What OC Rules Cannot Do

A blanket "no pets" rule is unenforceable under Victorian law. Similarly, rules that are so restrictive as to amount to a de facto ban — such as requiring all pets to be under two kilograms — are likely to be found unreasonable.

Rules that discriminate against specific breeds without a genuine safety basis, that require excessive documentation or approvals beyond what is reasonable, or that impose unreasonable financial charges on pet owners are also vulnerable to challenge.

### How This Affects Renters

As a renter, you are not directly a member of the OC — your landlord is. However, your landlord may cite OC rules as a reason for refusing your pet request. If the OC rules are reasonable restrictions (as opposed to a blanket ban), this can form part of a reasonable refusal.

In practice, if your landlord refuses your pet because of OC rules, ask to see the specific rules being relied upon. If the rules amount to a blanket ban or are unreasonably restrictive, they may not be enforceable, and the refusal based on them may not be reasonable.

If you are renting in an apartment building, checking the OC rules before submitting your pet request can help you tailor your application and anticipate potential objections. Your property manager should be able to provide the relevant OC rules on request. ## Practical Tips for Pet-Owning Renters

### Include Your Pet in Rental Applications

When applying for a rental property, mentioning your pet upfront is generally the best strategy. While you are not legally required to disclose a pet at the application stage (you can submit a formal request after signing the lease), transparency builds trust with landlords and agents.

Include a brief pet profile with your application: the pet's name, breed, age, temperament, and a photo. Mention that your pet is registered with the local council and up to date on vaccinations. If your pet has lived successfully in rental properties before, include a reference from a previous landlord or property manager.

### Create a Pet Resume

A pet resume might sound excessive, but it is one of the most effective tools for pet-owning renters in a competitive market. A simple one-page document that covers your pet's details, temperament, training history, vaccination records, and a photo can make a meaningful difference.

Include a brief section on how you manage your pet's behaviour — for example, noting that your dog is walked twice daily and attended obedience training, or that your cat is an indoor cat with a scratching post and litter tray.

Property managers see dozens of applications for popular listings. A professional pet resume signals that you are a responsible pet owner who takes the property seriously.

### Offer Proactive Commitments

In your pet request or rental application, consider offering commitments that address common landlord concerns before they arise. These might include committing to professional flea treatment at the end of the lease, offering to have carpets professionally steam-cleaned at the end of the tenancy, committing to promptly repairing any pet-related damage during the tenancy, and ensuring your pet is exercised regularly to minimise behavioural issues.

These commitments are not legally required, but they demonstrate responsibility and can tip a borderline decision in your favour.

### Document Everything

From the moment you submit your pet request, keep records of all communications. Save emails, take screenshots of text messages, and follow up phone conversations with a confirming email.

If your request is refused and you need to go to VCAT, having a clear paper trail of all communications makes your case significantly stronger. It also protects you against any claim that you did not follow the proper process.

### Know When to Seek Help

If you are uncertain about your rights, facing a refusal you believe is unreasonable, or experiencing retaliation after making a pet request, free help is available. The Tenants Union of Victoria provides advice and advocacy for Victorian renters. Consumer Affairs Victoria can provide information about the law and dispute resolution options.

Do not assume you have to navigate a difficult situation alone. The support services available to Victorian renters are well-established and experienced in pet-related disputes.

### Consider the Property Before You Apply

While the law protects your right to request a pet, being realistic about property suitability helps both you and your pet. A large, energetic dog breed will not thrive in a tiny studio apartment, and a request to keep such an animal in such a property may be reasonably refused.

When searching for rentals, look for properties that genuinely suit your pet's needs: a yard for dogs, quiet spaces for cats, and sufficient room for whatever animal you have. This approach increases the likelihood of approval and sets both you and your pet up for a better living experience.

If you are looking at renting from an individual rather than through an agency, the same rules apply. Our guide to [private landlord rentals in Melbourne](/guides/private-landlord-rentals-melbourne) covers the differences in practice.

## Common Scenarios and Outcomes

### Scenario 1 — Cat in a Two-Bedroom Apartment

Sarah rents a two-bedroom apartment in South Yarra and submits a written request to keep her domestic shorthair cat. The landlord refuses, citing concerns about carpet damage and potential odour issues.

**Likely outcome:** This refusal would almost certainly be overturned at VCAT. A single cat in a two-bedroom apartment is one of the most commonly approved pet requests. Cats are low-impact pets, and general concerns about carpet damage without specific evidence do not constitute a reasonable basis for refusal.

### Scenario 2 — Large Dog in a Studio Apartment

James rents a studio apartment in the CBD and requests permission to keep his adult Great Dane. The landlord refuses, citing the property's size and the dog's needs.

**Likely outcome:** This refusal has a reasonable basis. A Great Dane in a studio apartment raises genuine animal welfare concerns and practical space issues. VCAT would likely consider this a reasonable refusal. James might have more success requesting a smaller dog or looking for a more suitable property.

### Scenario 3 — Two Cats in a House

Maria rents a three-bedroom house in Reservoir and requests permission to keep two cats. The landlord refuses without providing any specific reasons.

**Likely outcome:** The refusal would almost certainly be overturned. Two cats in a three-bedroom house is entirely reasonable, and the landlord's failure to provide specific reasons strengthens Maria's position. The absence of reasons alone may render the refusal invalid.

### Scenario 4 — Dog in an Apartment with OC Restrictions

Tom rents a one-bedroom apartment in a complex where the OC rules require pets to be under 10 kilograms. Tom's dog weighs 15 kilograms. The landlord refuses the request, citing the OC rule.

**Likely outcome:** This is a genuinely contested scenario. VCAT would assess whether the 10-kilogram restriction is itself reasonable for the building. If the building has adequate common areas, the individual apartment is a reasonable size for the dog, and there is no evidence the dog would cause nuisance, the OC rule might be found unreasonable and the refusal overturned. However, if the building has small corridors, limited outdoor space, and legitimate structural or noise concerns, the rule might be upheld.

### Scenario 5 — Landlord Does Not Respond Within 14 Days

Priya submits a written pet request via email to her property manager. After 14 days, she has received no response.

**Likely outcome:** Consent is deemed to have been given. Priya is legally permitted to keep her pet. She should send a follow-up email confirming the deemed consent for her records. If the landlord later objects, Priya is on solid legal ground.

### Scenario 6 — Fish Tank in an Apartment

David wants to set up a 200-litre freshwater aquarium in his apartment. The landlord refuses, citing potential water damage.

**Likely outcome:** A standard aquarium of this size is generally considered a reasonable pet request. However, if the apartment is on an upper floor and the landlord can demonstrate genuine structural concerns about weight loading, there may be some basis for discussion. In most cases, a 200-litre tank is well within normal residential use and the refusal would be overturned.

### Scenario 7 — Getting a New Pet During an Existing Tenancy

Lisa has been renting a two-bedroom house in Preston for two years on a periodic (month-to-month) agreement. She decides to adopt a dog from a shelter and submits a pet request to her property manager.

**Likely outcome:** Lisa has every right to request a pet during an existing tenancy, whether on a fixed-term or periodic agreement. The same 14-day response process applies. If the dog is a reasonable size for the property, approval is likely. The landlord cannot use Lisa's request as grounds to issue a notice to vacate.

## Pet-Friendly Suburbs in Melbourne

### Eastern Suburbs

Suburbs like [Doncaster](/guides/suburb-guide-doncaster-rentals) are particularly well-suited for pet owners, with extensive parks including the 50-hectare Ruffey Lake Park offering walking trails and open spaces. Neighbouring suburbs like Box Hill and Blackburn also offer good park access, though rental competition near train stations is higher.

### Inner Suburbs

Many inner suburbs have excellent dog parks and off-leash areas despite their urban density. [Richmond](/guides/suburb-guide-richmond-rentals) benefits from its proximity to the Yarra River trail system, while suburbs like Northcote and Thornbury have dedicated off-leash parks and a pet-friendly café culture.

### Outer Suburbs

For renters with larger dogs or multiple pets, outer suburbs offer more space at lower rents. Areas in Melbourne's western and northern growth corridors typically have larger properties with yards, making them naturally more suitable for pet owners. Check the [cheapest suburbs to rent in Melbourne](/guides/cheapest-suburbs-to-rent-melbourne-2026) for options that combine affordability with space.

### What to Look For

When searching for a pet-friendly rental, prioritise proximity to parks and off-leash areas, properties with secure fencing (for dog owners), ground-floor apartments with courtyard access, and suburbs with pet-friendly local businesses and services.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can my landlord charge a pet bond in Victoria?**
No. Victorian law explicitly prohibits separate pet bonds or pet deposits. Your bond is capped at a maximum of four weeks' rent regardless of whether you keep a pet. However, your landlord can claim for genuine pet damage from your regular bond at the end of the tenancy if the damage exceeds normal wear and tear.

**What happens if my landlord doesn't respond to my pet request within 14 days?**
If your landlord does not respond in writing within 14 days of receiving your written pet request, consent is automatically deemed to have been given. You are legally permitted to keep the pet. Send a follow-up email confirming this for your records.

**Can I be evicted for having a pet in Victoria?**
If you have followed the correct process — submitted a written request and received consent (or deemed consent through non-response) — you cannot be evicted for having the pet. If you have a pet without going through the formal process, you may be in breach of your lease, though eviction is still subject to VCAT oversight and reasonableness requirements.

**How much does it cost to challenge a pet refusal at VCAT?**
The VCAT application fee for a residential tenancies matter is approximately $65 as of 2026. Fee waivers are available for concession card holders and those experiencing financial hardship. If VCAT finds the refusal unreasonable, the landlord may be ordered to reimburse your application fee.

**Do the pet laws apply to all rental properties in Victoria?**
The laws apply to all residential tenancies covered by the Residential Tenancies Act 1997, which includes the vast majority of private rental properties. Some exemptions may apply to specialist accommodation, rooming houses, and certain other housing types. If you are unsure whether your tenancy is covered, contact Consumer Affairs Victoria or the Tenants Union of Victoria.
